What's Next: Financial Markets and Institutions

From analyzing companies to understanding the markets they operate in

In FINAN I, you learned to analyze individual companies: reading their financial statements, calculating ratios, building projections, and making investment recommendations.

But here's what you haven't learned yet: How do financial markets actually work, and how do institutions make money from them?

When you buy a stock, who's on the other side of that trade? When interest rates rise, what happens to bond prices and why? How do investment banks, market makers, and hedge funds profit from facilitating transactions? What causes market crashes, and why do prices often overreact before reversing?

FINAN III: Financial Markets and Institutions teaches you to understand the system itself, not just individual companies within it.

What You'll Learn

Market Structure and Function

Understand how primary markets (where securities are created) differ from secondary markets (where they trade). Compare exchange-based trading (NYSE, NASDAQ) versus over-the-counter markets. Analyze why financial markets exist and what value they provide to participants.

Institutional Business Models

Investigate how banks, investment firms, and market makers generate revenue. Learn why Goldman Sachs makes money differently than Vanguard, and why JPMorgan's trading desk operates differently than its commercial banking division.

Interest Rates and Monetary Policy

Analyze what determines interest rates across different securities and time horizons. Interpret yield curve dynamics: why sometimes short-term rates exceed long-term rates, and what that signals about economic conditions. Evaluate how Federal Reserve policy tools affect capital allocation and market pricing.

Securities Markets

Compare money markets (short-term debt), bond markets (long-term debt), mortgage markets (securitized loans), stock markets (equity), and foreign exchange markets (currencies). Understand the purpose and behavior of financial instruments within each market.

Market Dynamics and Price Discovery

Examine how geopolitical events, economic announcements, and market sentiment affect asset prices. Analyze why markets often overreact initially then reverse course. Understand the difference between fundamental value and market price.

Trading Mechanisms

Compare high-frequency trading, algorithmic trading, and traditional trading methods. Analyze their impact on market efficiency, liquidity, and individual investors.

How It Builds on FINAN I

Everything you learned analyzing individual companies becomes the foundation for analyzing markets:

Financial statement mastery (Units 4-6): You'll analyze how institutions' business models appear in their financials
Ratio analysis and trends (Units 7-8): You'll apply these tools to evaluate institutional performance and market behavior
Excel and data analysis (Units 1-2): You'll build models analyzing interest rate movements, yield curves, and trading strategies
Investment analysis (Units 9-13): You'll expand from "Is this company good?" to "How is this market behaving and why?"

The Question Changes

FINAN I taught you to ask: "Should I buy this stock?"

FINAN III teaches you to ask: "How does this market work, why is this price moving, and how are institutions profiting from this system?"

Both questions matter. Both are essential for finance professionals. FINAN I gave you company analysis tools. FINAN III gives you market analysis tools.

Real-World Application

These concepts matter for:

  • Investment roles: Understanding why markets behave the way they do helps you evaluate not just which securities to buy, but when to buy them and what market conditions to watch
  • Banking careers: Knowing how different financial institutions make money helps you understand business strategy and competitive positioning
  • Corporate finance: Understanding how markets price securities helps you evaluate financing options and capital structure decisions
  • Personal investing: Market structure knowledge helps you avoid common mistakes (trading during panic, chasing momentum, misunderstanding risk)
